## 1. INTRODUCTION: DIAGNOSIS, PARADOX, AND NEUROBIOLOGICAL BASIS

### The Scale of the Problem

Research from 2024–2026 paints a consistent and alarming picture. The integration of generative AI into everyday professional and educational activities has reached a saturation point, beyond which systemic cognitive consequences begin to emerge.

In the MIT Media Lab study (2025), using EEG, 54 participants performed academic tasks in three groups: independently, with Google search, and with ChatGPT. ChatGPT users showed the worst results — they exhibited reduced activity in brain regions associated with memory, and decreased neural network connectivity in alpha and beta bands [1]. The best results were demonstrated by those who used neither AI nor search engines; they showed the highest level of neural connections, especially in areas responsible for speech comprehension, creativity, and memory [1]. The researchers concluded that frequent use of AI assistants may lead to "skill atrophy" in problem-solving [1].

The Microsoft and Carnegie Mellon University study (2025), covering 319 knowledge workers and 936 task examples, showed that higher confidence in the tool's ability to perform a task correlates with "less critical thinking effort" [2]. While generative AI can improve efficiency, it can suppress critical engagement with work and potentially lead to long-term over-reliance on the tool and a decline in independent problem-solving skills [2].

The Wharton School study (2026), involving over 200 students, showed that providing learners with on-demand AI assistance undermines "productive struggle" — the cognitively demanding work of overcoming difficulties that is critical for long-term skill development. Participants with access to on-demand AI assistance showed only 30% performance gains, compared to 64% for participants with controlled, automatic assistance [3]. Although participants knew that excessive use of assistance harmed learning, by the end of the study they were requesting hints every 3–4 moves, effectively delegating decision-making to AI [3]. The researchers identified three major shifts in cognitive strategies: from information gathering to verification, from problem-solving to integrating AI responses, and from task execution to "task stewardship" [3].

The Boston Consulting Group and University of California study (2026) identified a condition called "AI brain fry" — cognitive burnout from excessive AI use. Among 1,488 participants, 14% reported symptoms: difficulty focusing, slowed decision-making, headaches. The researchers defined this condition as "mental fatigue from excessive use or oversight of AI tools beyond one's cognitive capacity" [4].

The UBC study (2025), published in PNAS Nexus, showed that a two-week mobile internet shutdown improved participants' sustained attention to the level of people ten years younger. Most participants reported improvements in mental health, focus, and emotional calm [5].

### The AI Paradox

An integrative review of empirical studies formulates the central contradiction as the *"efficiency-atrophy paradox"*: digital tools optimize short-term task performance at the cost of potential harm to long-term cognitive effort necessary for maintaining independent thinking. While research on earlier technologies mainly described disruptions in attention allocation, early data on generative AI indicate erosion of higher-order cognitive abilities and metacognitive capacities [6].

As noted in Nature (January 2026), neuroplasticity is shaped by how people interact with AI: passive, uncritical dependence can weaken activity-dependent neuroplasticity and impair cognition, while active co-creation can support or even enhance it [7]. Researchers distinguish three forms of cognitive offloading: *assistive* (helps but does not interfere), *substitutive* (replaces one's own thinking), and *destructive* (forms passive interaction with loss of cognitive control) [7].

The study by Fan et al. (2025), published in the British Journal of Educational Technology, confirms that excessive delegation to AI systems reduces such fundamental human abilities as memory retention, critical thinking, metacognitive awareness, creativity, and professional judgment. The authors introduce the concept of "metacognitive laziness" — the tendency to rely on AI without fully engaging in thinking [8].

### Neurobiological Basis: Why This Happens

Modern neuroscience offers an explanation for these phenomena through the concept of neuroplasticity — the brain's ability to modify synaptic efficiency, form new neural connections, and reorganize networks throughout life [9]. According to the "use it or lose it" principle, plasticity is an active phenomenon that must be maintained through consistent engagement in a given cognitive function [10].

**The metaphor of neural bridges and highways.** Thinking about something builds thin neural connections ("bridges"). The more often and deeply a person thinks about a topic, the denser and wider these connections become, turning into high-speed "highways." The presence of dense highways enables rapid automatic pattern recognition and decision-making — this is the neurobiological basis of expertise. However, excessive automation leads to the opposite effect: new information that does not fit into existing highways "slides off," bypassing critical analysis blocks. A person stops noticing alternatives, perceiving habitual assumptions as constants. When one neural highway becomes dominant, it suppresses alternative paths, leading to cognitive rigidity.

**The neural correlate of cognitive degradation.** Passive, uncritical interaction with AI, where the user accepts the first answer as truth without verification and reflection, leads to weakening of activity-dependent neuroplasticity [7]. The brain adapts to reduced cognitive load just as a muscle adapts to its absence — gradual cognitive atrophy [7].

### Why This Guide Is Necessary

A significant portion of users (according to researchers' estimates) use AI as a substitute for thinking rather than as an amplifier of it. The result of this practice, confirmed by numerous empirical studies, is a decline in critical thinking, atrophy of writing skills, loss of "cognitive patience," illusion of competence, and professional rigidity.

At the same time, review works show that the impact of AI on cognitive abilities is not intrinsically positive or negative: it depends on pedagogical design, AI literacy, and the degree of agency that users retain. The key factor distinguishing development from degradation is the *mode of interaction* [11].

**The purpose of this guide:** to transform AI from an "autopilot" that leads to cognitive degradation into a "co-pilot" and intellectual trainer that preserves and enhances the user's cognitive abilities.

## 2. 5 GOLDEN RULES + ONE-PAGE CHEAT SHEET

*Recommended to print on A4 and place above your workspace.*

---

### Rule 1: AI is a Tool, Not an Author

**Neurobiological basis:** The MIT Media Lab study (2025) showed that ChatGPT users exhibited the lowest neural activity in regions associated with memory, language processing, and attention. Participants who performed tasks without using AI showed the strongest and most widespread neural activity [1].

**Principle:** When you delegate to AI not only execution but also the *process of thinking* (generation of hypotheses, structuring arguments, formulating conclusions), substitutive cognitive offloading occurs — AI replaces the user's own thinking.

| # | Rule | ❌ Prohibited Prompt Format | ✅ Recommended Prompt Format |
|---|------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| 1 | AI is a tool, not an author | "Write an essay on… 500 words, conclusion." | "Here are my theses. Find logical gaps, ask 3 counter-questions, propose an alternative point of view." |

---

### Rule 2: Cognitive Friction Is Necessary

**Neurobiological basis:** The Wharton School study (2026) showed that providing learners with on-demand AI assistance undermines "productive struggle" — the cognitively demanding work of overcoming difficulties critical for long-term skill development [3]. Students with on-demand AI assistance showed only 30% gains, compared to 64% for participants with controlled assistance [3].

**Principle:** "Desirable difficulties" — cognitive friction, pauses, reformulations — build neural bridges, while smoothness and speed lead to cognitive atrophy.

| # | Rule | ❌ Prohibited Prompt Format | ✅ Recommended Prompt Format |
|---|------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| 2 | Cognitive friction is necessary | "Give a brief summary without examples and context." | "Break down the concept layer by layer. Leave key formulations for my own rephrasing. Indicate where beginners most often make mistakes." |

---

### Rule 3: Speed ≠ Understanding

**Neurobiological basis:** The MIT Media Lab study showed that when participants were asked to reproduce previously written text without AI assistance, ChatGPT users could not remember what they had written, while participants who used only their own brain demonstrated strong recall ability. Even after ceasing ChatGPT use, former users retained lower brain activity [1].

**Principle:** The speed that AI provides is a short-term loan against long-term memory.

| # | Rule | ❌ Prohibited Prompt Format | ✅ Recommended Prompt Format |
|---|------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| 3 | Speed ≠ understanding | "Fix all errors in the text." | "Indicate lines with errors or stylistic issues. Explain the rule. I will fix it myself." |

---

### Rule 4: First You – Then Machine

**Neurobiological basis:** The MIT study revealed a key pattern: participants who first wrote independently and only then used ChatGPT maintained higher cognitive engagement. However, when the model was used first, a form of "metacognitive laziness" emerged [1]. The study by Fan et al. (2025) confirms this phenomenon: students who trusted AI and used it regularly reported significantly lower cognitive engagement [8].

**Principle:** Using AI *after* you have formed your own thoughts turns it from a substitute into an amplifier of cognitive processes.

| # | Rule | ❌ Prohibited Prompt Format | ✅ Recommended Prompt Format |
|---|------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| 4 | First you – then machine | "Check if my hypothesis is correct." | "Play the role of a skeptic. Find 3 weak points in my argumentation and propose an experiment to test it." |

---

### Rule 5: Sovereignty of Attention

**Neurobiological basis:** The Nature study (2026) shows that the key factor distinguishing development from degradation is the *mode of interaction* [7]. When users already have solid knowledge in a domain, AI can enhance engagement and support innovation rather than suppress it.

**Principle:** Always retain final decision-making, pace, and depth of work. Start with your own draft.

| # | Rule | ❌ Prohibited Prompt Format | ✅ Recommended Prompt Format |
|---|------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| 5 | Sovereignty of attention | Any prompt that transfers control to the algorithm | Always retain final decision-making, pace, and depth of work. Start with your own draft. |

## 3. PROMPT PROTOCOLS: WHAT TO SAY AND WHAT TO AVOID

*Color coding: 🔴 prohibition → degradation, 🟢 allowed → development*

| 🔴 Leads to degradation | 🟢 Develops thinking |
|---|---|
| "Do everything for me: write an essay / solve a problem / make a plan." | "Show the structure / methodology of the solution; I will fill in the content and make the conclusions myself." |
| "Evaluate my work according to criteria X." | "Point out 2 strengths and 2 weaknesses in my work, ask 3 clarifying questions that will help me improve it myself." |
| "Rewrite it to sound professional / grammatically correct." | "Suggest 3 synonymous variations for this paragraph. Explain how they differ in meaning and style. I will choose and adapt to the context." |
| "Give a brief summary without details." | "Break down the concept layer by layer: from basic principles to complex nuances. Leave key formulations unfilled — I will fill them myself, checking my understanding." |
| "Is my solution / hypothesis correct?" | "Play the role of a skeptic. Find 3 weak points in my argumentation. Propose an experiment or a way to test my hypothesis." |
| "Fix all errors in the text / code." | "Indicate lines where there are potential errors or stylistic issues. Explain the rule / principle that is violated. I will fix it myself." |
| "Do it like in example X." | "Show how it's done on another example (not mine), with an explanation of each step. I will repeat on my own task and show the result." |

## 4. ROLE-SPECIFIC RECOMMENDATIONS

### 4.1. For Students and Learners

**Main risks:** Illusion of competence, loss of writing skills, dependence on ready-made answers, suppression of metacognitive regulation.

**Research:** The Tomsk State University study (2025) with 60 students showed that using generative AI reduces the actual level of creativity, while creating an **illusion of one's own creative productivity**. Participants working with AI demonstrated higher subjective self-assessment, but objective creativity indicators were lower than in the group working without AI [12].

**Rules for students:**

1. **First draft rule.** Before any AI query, write one paragraph, outline a structure or diagram in your own words — without technology assistance.

2. **24-hour rule.** After completing a task with AI, set it aside for a day. Then retell the key ideas in your own words without referring to the source. This activates the spacing effect and memory consolidation.

3. **Replace "do for me" with "show me how it's done so I can repeat it myself."**

**Allowed prompt formats for students:**

| 🟢 Allowed | Rationale |
|---|---|
| "Help me come up with 3 questions to this text for self-testing." | Develops metacognitive monitoring |
| "Check if I explained this concept clearly in my own words. Point out where I am mistaken or oversimplifying." | Preserves agency and activates retrieval practice |
| "Compare the two approaches to solving the problem that I described. What are the advantages and disadvantages of each?" | Develops analytical thinking |
| "Find a weakness in my plan, ask 2 clarifying questions." | Trains critical evaluation of one's own decisions |

---

### 4.2. For Teachers and Educators

**Main risks:** Delegation of assessment, loss of control over skill development, blind trust in AI as a source of educational materials.

**Research:** A 2025 review shows that GenAI can both support the development of metacognitive skills and provoke "metacognitive laziness" and cognitive passivity [11].

**Rules for teachers:**

1. **Shift focus from product to process.** Evaluate drafts, argumentation logic, reflection, prompt history.

2. **Process evaluation criteria (scale 0–3):**
   - `0` — work copied, no traces of processing
   - `1` — draft exists, minimal edits
   - `2` — draft + list of questions to AI + brief reflection
   - `3` — draft + prompt history with explanations + reflection

3. **Introduce assignments where AI is a mandatory opponent.** For example: "Use AI to find 3 counterarguments to your thesis, and refute them in your essay."

4. **Don't ban AI outright. Legalize it with rules.** "AI is allowed at the stage of searching for structure and counterarguments, prohibited at the stage of formulating conclusions and final editing."

---

### 4.3. For Academics and Researchers

**Main risks:** Professional rigidity, confirmation bias, hallucinations in citations, loss of ability to generate hypotheses independently.

**Research:** A 2025 study with 28 pathology experts revealed that AI integration can fuel confirmation bias: experts who received AI recommendations were more likely to agree with them, even when they were erroneous [13].

**Rules for researchers:**

1. **Anti-hallucination protocol (3 steps):**
   - **Step 1.** Request sources with DOI/URL. Use the prompt: "Don't fabricate DOI. Write 'Verify manually:' followed by author list, year, title."
   - **Step 2.** Quick verification in Google Scholar / Crossref.
   - **Step 3.** Read and cite **only from the primary source**.

2. **Confirmation bias protection protocol:** Before querying AI, formulate your hypothesis and an alternative hypothesis. Then ask AI: "Find 3 pieces of evidence against my hypothesis and 2 in support of the alternative."

3. **Two-source rule:** Never accept information from AI without verifying it in two independent primary sources.

---

### 4.4. For Leaders and Team Leads

**Main risks:** Delegation of reflection, homogenization of decisions, loss of expertise, cognitive overload.

**Research:** A study published in the Journal of Applied Psychology (2025) showed that prolonged AI use is significantly associated with mental exhaustion, attention load, and information overload [14]. Leaders who rely more on AI report higher levels of cognitive fatigue.

**Rules for leaders:**

1. **Two-opinion rule:** Any significant conclusion obtained with AI must be refuted or confirmed by an employee who did not use AI for that task.

2. **Implement "no-AI hours":** first 2 hours of the workday for brainstorming and key decisions without technological support.

3. **Cognitive safety KPIs for the team:**
   - `% of tasks with mandatory draft before AI` (target: ≥60%)
   - `Solution diversity index` — variance of approaches in the team
   - `Surprise coefficient` — proportion of AI responses that elicited "Hmm, I didn't consider that" (>20%)

## 5. CHECKLIST: 7 SIGNS OF COGNITIVE DEGRADATION

*With neural markers, self-assessment scale, and scientific rationale*

---

### How to Use This Checklist

Mark ☐ each item that manifests **regularly (over the last 2–3 weeks)**. Use the self-assessment scale (1–5) after each item.

> ⚠️ **Important note.** Cognitive degradation associated with AI use is a measurable decline in memory, attention, speed and quality of thinking, not reaching the threshold of clinical dementia. It is a reversible process that depends on the *mode* of interaction with AI.

---

### Sign 1. I Accept the First AI Answer as Truth Without Follow-Up

**Symptom:** You don't ask clarifying questions, don't request sources, don't verify facts.

**Neural marker:** Reduced activity in the prefrontal cortex (responsible for critical thinking). The MIT study (2025) showed that in ChatGPT users, the complex network of neural pathways systematically decreases as the volume of external support increases [1].

**Self-assessment scale (1–5):**
> 1 = Never — always verify and cross-check
> 2 = Rarely
> 3 = Sometimes
> 4 = Often
> 5 = Always — accept without doubt

---

### Sign 2. I Avoid Texts Longer Than 3–5 Pages

**Symptom:** You find it difficult to read long, complex texts without breaking them into parts. You "scan" the text instead of reading.

**Neural marker:** Weakening of connectivity in the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and posterior cingulate cortex — regions responsible for context maintenance. MIT researchers note: "the complex network of neural pathways systematically decreases with increasing volume of external support" [1].

**Self-assessment scale (1–5):**
> 1 = I read complex texts without difficulty
> 2 = Sometimes lose the thread but can return
> 3 = Notice I've started reading more superficially
> 4 = Require brief summaries instead of full texts
> 5 = Don't read long texts at all

---

### Sign 3. I Can't Retell Material in My Own Words

**Symptom:** You understand the material while you see it, but when trying to retell it you "freeze," need prompts.

**Neural marker:** Reduced activity in the hippocampus (memory consolidation center). The MIT study showed: participants who used ChatGPT could not reproduce their own essays, while participants without AI reproduced texts almost verbatim [1].

**Self-assessment scale (1–5):**
> 1 = I retell easily
> 2 = Retell with minor losses
> 3 = Can retell but need structure
> 4 = Difficult, lose key points
> 5 = Can't retell without returning to source

---

### Sign 4. Basic Literacy Mistakes Become Habitual

**Symptom:** You notice you're making more spelling mistakes, errors in simple calculations.

**Neural marker:** Atrophy of automated neural patterns in procedural memory areas (basal ganglia, cerebellum).

**Self-assessment scale (1–5):**
> 1 = I don't notice mistakes
> 2 = Rare mistakes, but I notice them
> 3 = Mistakes have become noticeably more frequent
> 4 = Regularly make mistakes in simple things
> 5 = Constantly need AI verification for basics

---

### Sign 5. I Feel Anxious When AI Is Unavailable

**Symptom:** You experience discomfort, anxiety when you can't use AI.

**Neural marker:** Increased activity in the amygdala (anxiety center) and reduced inhibitory control from the prefrontal cortex.

**Self-assessment scale (1–5):**
> 1 = I feel confident without AI
> 2 = Mild discomfort, but adapt quickly
> 3 = Noticeable anxiety, distracted
> 4 = Severe anxiety, difficulty concentrating
> 5 = Panic, can't work without AI

---

### Sign 6. I Stopped Writing Drafts Without Autocomplete

**Symptom:** Any task starts with a query to AI. Your own draft has ceased to exist.

**Neural marker:** Suppression of creative and generative neural networks (default mode network). MIT research showed: when AI was used first, a form of "metacognitive laziness" emerged [1].

**Self-assessment scale (1–5):**
> 1 = Always start with my own draft
> 2 = Mostly start myself, sometimes use AI
> 3 = Half of tasks start with AI
> 4 = Most tasks start with AI
> 5 = Never start without AI

---

### Sign 7. I Use AI to Confirm My Views, Not to Challenge Them

**Symptom:** You use AI to get confirmation of what you already think.

**Neural marker:** Strengthening of confirmation bias through dopamine reinforcement upon receiving confirming answers.

**Self-assessment scale (1–5):**
> 1 = Actively seek refutations and alternatives
> 2 = Sometimes ask for counterarguments
> 3 = More often seek confirmations, but check myself
> 4 = Mostly use AI for confirmation
> 5 = Only confirmation, ignore counterarguments

## 6. TRAINERS FOR MAINTAINING NEUROPLASTICITY

*Neurobiological basis.* The brain builds neural "bridges" that, with frequent use, turn into high-speed "highways." If one highway becomes dominant, it suppresses alternative paths. The trainers below aim to shake up dominant highways and build multiple connections of varying strength.

| Exercise | How to Perform | Neurobiological Goal |
|---|---|---|
| **Blind retelling** | Study text → close AI and sources → retell/write from memory → compare with original, note discrepancies | Activates retrieval practice. Builds dense bridges, preventing AI from replacing memory |
| **Devil's advocate** | Formulate a thesis. AI gives 3 counterarguments. You counter each. Repeat 3 rounds. End: has your position changed and why? | Shakes confirmation bias and professional rigidity. Forced building of alternative neural pathways |
| **Manual verification** | Receiving an AI answer, find 3 primary sources (scientific articles, official data). Verify each fact. Make manual edits. Format citations | Direct combat against AI hallucinations. Strengthens source work and critical evaluation skills |
| **Long reading with pencil** | 20 min/day: take a complex text from another professional field. Make margin notes (questions, objections, analogies). Close text, retell main idea | Trains "cognitive patience" — ability to hold complex context. Expands horizons through exposure to areas beyond your competence |
| **Prompt reflection** | Keep a journal for each AI session: what I wanted → what I got → where I agree/disagree → what I changed in my original position → how I'll change the next prompt | Metacognitive control — awareness of one's own cognitive processes. Counteracts illusion of competence and delegation of reflection |
| **Highway deautomatization** | Take a task in which you are an expert. Forbid yourself professional jargon and automatic solutions. Describe it as a smart 12-year-old would. Or solve it deliberately using a "wrong" approach | Shakes the dominant highway. Forced building of alternative paths suppressed by automation. Direct countermeasure to the Einstellung effect |
| **Metacognitive pause** | Before each AI query, stop for 60 seconds. Write answers to three questions: "What exactly do I want to know?", "What do I already know about this topic?", "What is the most important question I should ask?" | Activates your own neural bridges *before* turning to AI. Prevents automatic delegation and creates a "draft before AI" |
| **Naive question** | Ask AI: "Play the role of someone who has never heard of my profession. Ask 5 naive questions about my solution/hypothesis." Answer each in writing | Deliberate exit from professional rut into a different realm of perception. Builds bridges where professional highways have created blind spots |

## 7. COGNITIVE DETOX PROTOCOL

*Graded program for cognitive function recovery*

---

### Why This Is Needed

Cognitive detox is a **deliberate, structured reduction of AI and technology use** to restore natural cognitive mechanisms.

Research from 2025–2026 shows that even short-term abstinence from digital stimuli leads to significant cognitive improvements:

- **Two weeks without internet** on a smartphone improved participants' sustained attention to the level of people ten years younger [5].
- **Four weeks of short-video restriction** improved neural activity, reduced cognitive overload and fatigue [15].
- **A systematic review of studies** confirmed that digital detox offers cognitive and emotional benefits: improved attention, reduced stress, and enhanced self-reflection [16].

---

### How to Use the Protocol

1. **Complete the checklist from Section 5** to determine your current level.
2. **Select the appropriate detox level** from the table.
3. **Follow the steps** for the chosen level for the specified duration.
4. **Keep an abstinence journal** (Appendix 3).
5. **Upon completion,** conduct a final assessment.

---

### Choosing Your Detox Level

| Level | Duration | AI Mode | For Whom |
|---|---|---|---|
| 🟢 **Light** | 2 days per week | Only fact-checking (with verification) | Score 7–14, prevention |
| 🟡 **Standard** | 7–14 days | Only assistive offloading | Score 15–21 |
| 🔴 **Intensive** | 14–21 days | Complete refusal of generation | Score 22+ |

---

### 🟢 Light Protocol (2 days per week)

**Prohibited:**
- Generation of texts, code, plans by AI
- Using AI for decision-making
- Autocomplete and autofill in writing

**Allowed:**
- Fact and data search (with verification through 1–2 primary sources)
- Spelling check (with rule explanation)
- Search for links to scientific articles

**Daily practice:**
- 30 minutes of deep reading without digital devices
- Written reflection at the end of the day
- Metacognitive pause before each AI use

---

### 🟡 Standard Protocol (7–14 days)

**Prohibited:**
- ALL forms of generative AI
- Search engines with AI summaries
- Auto-translation and auto-correction

**Allowed:**
- Classical search engines for accessing primary sources
- Dictionaries and reference books (without AI summaries)
- Manual spelling check

**Daily practice:**
- 45–60 minutes of deep reading
- Daily writing without technology (at least 300 words)
- Memory training: retelling after reading
- Desire journal: each time the urge to turn to AI arises

---

### 🔴 Intensive Protocol (14–21 days)

**For whom:** Participants in the BCG study experiencing AI brain fry — difficulty focusing, slowed decision-making, headaches [4].

**Prohibited (absolutely everything):**
- Complete refusal of generative AI in all forms
- Complete refusal of search engines with AI components
- Complete refusal of autocomplete, autocorrection

**Allowed:**
- Paper books, magazines, newspapers
- Handwriting (by hand)
- Offline dictionaries, encyclopedias
- Communication with people (live)
- Nature, physical activity

**Daily practice (minimum):**
- 2 hours of deep reading without digital devices
- 1 hour of handwriting
- 30 minutes of cognitive exercises

## 8. CONCLUSION: AUTOPILOT OR HELM?

### 8.1. Consciousness is not just “level of activity”

Our empirical research (cross-population validation) shows that the φ index, reflecting the formalisability of consciousness, is specific to clinical disorders and does not generalise to sleep. This emphasises that consciousness is not merely an “activity level” or “signal complexity”, but a complex integrative process that can be disrupted in different ways.

Similarly, in everyday AI use, we must distinguish between:
- **Temporary reduction in cognitive load** (rest, sleep) — this is normal and necessary.
- **Systemic degradation of thinking skills** (AI dependence, metacognitive laziness) — this is a pathology requiring intervention.

Principles 2 (Cognitive friction is necessary) and 4 (First you – then machine) are specifically aimed at preventing systemic degradation, not at avoiding rest. Rest is recovery, not delegation of thinking to AI.

### Two Paths

Research from 2024–2026 paints a consistent picture. Generative AI is not neutral. It is an **amplifier of the cognitive position** you adopt.

**The consumer path (autopilot):**
- Delegation of thinking → illusion of competence → skill atrophy → dependence → cognitive rigidity → symptoms of AI brain fry [4].
- Neural correlate: suppression of neural activity in memory and critical thinking regions [1].

**The architect path (helm):**
- Your own draft → dialogue with AI as opponent → critique and verification → appropriation of knowledge → cognitive flexibility [1].
- Neural correlate: building multiple neural connections, maintaining neuroplasticity [7].

### Three Main Takeaways from Research

1. **Efficiency ≠ understanding.** The MIT study showed that ChatGPT users wrote faster but could not reproduce their own texts minutes later [1].

2. **Productive struggle is necessary.** The Wharton School study showed that students with on-demand AI assistance showed only 30% gains vs. 64% for those with controlled assistance [3].

3. **AI dependence is real and measurable.** Participants, knowing that excessive help harms learning, requested hints every 3–4 moves by the end of the study [3]. The BCG study identified "AI brain fry" with symptoms of cognitive burnout [4].

### What You Take Away

**Your daily personal checklist:**

☐ Do I start with my own draft?  
☐ Do I use AI as an opponent, not as confirmation?  
☐ Do I retain final decision-making?  
☐ Do I pause for metacognitive reflection?  
☐ Do I verify facts from AI against primary sources?  
☐ Do I practice "blind retelling" and long reading?  
☐ Do I take the checklist every 2–3 weeks?

### Final Metaphor

Where your effort ends, your intellect ends.

Neuroplasticity requires activity. A brain accustomed to receiving ready-made answers stops building new bridges. It glides along old highways, becoming faster in the familiar and blind to the new.

AI can be your co-pilot, trainer, opponent — anything you want. But it should never be your thinking "self."

Because **where autopilot begins, the pilot ends**.
